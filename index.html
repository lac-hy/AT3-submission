EXPLORING MELBOURNE’S
RHYTHM THROUGH SOUND COMM2753

The project transforms Melbourne's landmarks and urban rhythms into an immersive auditory experience

I wanted to explore how different parts of a city carry their own distinct sonic identities—how the sounds of movement, architecture, and atmosphere shape our perception of place. Through an experimental approach, I aimed to translate these urban sound textures into an interactive experience, allowing users to listen to the city as a living composition. The project became a way to express the relationship between environment and emotion through sound, design, and digital interaction.
01

How I Envisioned The Project.

The Thought Process Behind The Creation

The guiding values of my design centred on immersion, intuition, and exploration. I wanted the interface to feel like an extension of the soundscape itself but rich in atmosphere. Every visual and interactive element was designed to reflect the project’s central idea: that sound can shape how we experience place. In designing the feedback and information hierarchy, I focused on creating an interface that communicates clearly without interrupting the sensory experience. Visual feedback was intentionally soft — hover animations, opacity shifts, and sound-trigger cues — giving users confirmation without distraction.

Mapping interactions were designed with learnability in mind: users are encouraged to explore intuitively, discovering patterns and relationships through play rather than instruction. Overall, the design’s simplicity allows the experience to breathe — inviting curiosity and reflection while maintaining focus on the auditory dimension.

02

Making Significant UI Changes.

Significant Updates I Made Since Milestone 2

Since my work in the second progress milestone, I made several refinements to both the visual and interactive design of the site. I shifted to a cohesive system that mirrored the aesthetic of my reference design more closely. I also improved the UI capabilties of the website, the main goal since my previous milestone was incorporating the intended sound/music function into the project. I intended to give each point within Melbourne it's own unique & signature melody / beat / rhythm. This transformed the project from a simple interactive map into an experimental auditory experience. The system supports multiple simultaneous sounds, meaning users can layer and mix various locations to create their own version of Melbourne’s soundscape. Technically, this required rewriting parts of my event-handling logic so multiple audio sources could play and stop independently. I also integrated volume controls for subtle variation in playback, giving the experience a more organic and evolving quality. Conceptually, this shifted the project from a visual-only map into an interactive composition tool, allowing users to literally “play” the city as an instrument.

After achieving the core map functionality, I dedicated most of my later development time to the sound engine, testing, debugging, and refining how sounds triggered and overlapped. A large portion of my process involved experimenting with different file types, adjusting gain values, and preventing abrupt cuts. Once the sound system was stable, I focused on syncing the interaction design with the auditory feedback, ensuring animations, hover states, and fades aligned with the rhythm of the audio.
